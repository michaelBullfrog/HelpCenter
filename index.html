Control Hub

Administration, identity, users, licensing, roles, locations, workspaces, analytics, and service management.

Control Hub overview

  • Control Hub is the central administrative portal for Webex services, users, roles, locations, workspaces, devices, analytics, and troubleshooting.
  • Use it to manage Webex Calling, Meetings, Messaging, Contact Center access, devices, hybrid services, and organization-level settings.
  • For support, always confirm whether the issue is caused by user configuration, licensing, role permissions, location settings, network conditions, or service health.

Admin role types

  • Full administrators can broadly manage organization settings.
  • Read-only administrators are useful for audits and support visibility.
  • Support administrators are useful for troubleshooting without full configuration rights.
  • Location administrators should be used when a user only manages specific locations.
  • Device administrators focus on device and workspace management.
  • Compliance roles should be limited and reviewed carefully.

User lifecycle checklist

  • Create or synchronize the user.
  • Confirm email address and identity status.
  • Apply license templates or manually assign licenses.
  • Assign location and calling details if needed.
  • Assign Contact Center profiles, teams, and privileges if applicable.
  • Send or resend activation email if required.
  • Validate Webex App sign-in and service access.

Licensing checklist

  • Confirm the subscription contains the needed service.
  • Confirm the user has the right license type.
  • Check license templates for automatic assignment behavior.
  • Review whether Calling, Meetings, Messaging, Contact Center, and device entitlements are all needed.
  • Remove stale licenses from inactive users to preserve capacity.

Groups and templates

  • Use groups to simplify license assignment and policy control.
  • Confirm group membership is accurate before troubleshooting missing services.
  • When using directory sync or identity provider groups, validate the sync source before changing users manually.
  • Document which groups drive licenses and which groups drive policies.

Locations best practices

  • Use consistent location names, such as City - Office Name.
  • Confirm the physical address before enabling Calling and emergency services.
  • Document PSTN provider, emergency calling settings, devices, numbers, and schedules per location.
  • Use locations to organize devices, workspaces, numbers, and regional administration.

Workspaces

  • Use workspaces for shared rooms, lobbies, conference rooms, warehouses, breakrooms, and common-area devices.
  • Confirm whether the workspace needs Calling, Meetings, device calendar integration, or just room/device management.
  • For Calling workspaces, assign a valid location, number or extension, and supported device.

Analytics and troubleshooting

  • Use Control Hub analytics for adoption, quality, service usage, and operational trends.
  • Use troubleshooting views for meeting, call, and device investigations when available.
  • For live issues, collect examples before data ages out or becomes harder to correlate.
  • Compare affected users with unaffected users in the same location, network, or device type.

Change management

  • Before making changes, capture current settings or screenshots.
  • Document the reason for change, affected users, planned rollback, and validation steps.
  • After changes, place a controlled test call or service test.
  • For customer environments, avoid undocumented changes during production hours unless there is an urgent business impact.

Calling

Calling setup workflow

  • Confirm location and emergency address.
  • Configure PSTN or confirm provider configuration.
  • Add or port phone numbers.
  • Assign licenses to users.
  • Assign numbers and extensions.
  • Add devices or Webex App calling.
  • Configure features such as auto attendants, call queues, hunt groups, schedules, voicemail, and call routing.

PSTN and number planning

  • Document the PSTN provider and location model.
  • Track main numbers, DIDs, toll-free numbers, fax numbers, and test numbers.
  • Keep a number inventory with assignment, location, feature owner, and notes.
  • Before porting, validate losing carrier details, billing telephone number, service address, and authorized signer.

Extensions and dialing plan

  • Use a consistent extension length where possible.
  • Avoid overlapping extensions across locations unless the design explicitly supports it.
  • Document extension ranges by site, department, or function.
  • Validate internal dialing, external dialing, emergency dialing, and transfer behavior.

Auto attendants

  • Use auto attendants for menu-based routing.
  • Document business hours, holiday schedules, menu options, timeout behavior, and invalid-entry handling.
  • Test open, closed, holiday, and after-hours scenarios.
  • Keep prompt scripts stored with the configuration notes.

Call queues

  • Use call queues when callers should wait for available staff.
  • Document agents, supervisors, queue policy, routing pattern, overflow, stranded calls, night service, holiday service, comfort messages, and music-on-hold behavior.
  • For support issues, confirm whether agents are signed in, available, and assigned correctly.
  • Test forced forwarding for emergency routing plans.

Hunt groups

  • Use hunt groups for simple ring patterns where full queue treatment is not required.
  • Document ring pattern, members, forwarding behavior, and no-answer handling.
  • Use call queues instead when you need comfort messages, queue policies, supervisors, or deeper reporting.

Schedules and holidays

  • Create reusable schedules for business hours and holidays.
  • Confirm timezone for each schedule.
  • Validate whether schedules apply to auto attendants, call queues, call forwarding, or operating modes.
  • Test holiday routing before the holiday date.

Voicemail

  • Confirm whether voicemail belongs to a user, group, queue, or shared function.
  • Document greeting type, notification settings, message delivery, and retention expectations.
  • For support, test direct-to-voicemail, no-answer voicemail, and after-hours voicemail routing separately.

Call forwarding and routing

  • Check user forwarding, feature forwarding, after-hours routing, selective forwarding, and emergency/forced forwarding.
  • Trace the call path from number to destination.
  • Document each hop: main number, auto attendant, queue, user, voicemail, external transfer, or Contact Center entry point.

Virtual lines and shared functions

  • Use virtual lines when a line identity needs to appear on multiple devices or users.
  • Document which users/devices have the line, who owns it operationally, and expected inbound/outbound caller ID behavior.
  • Validate emergency calling and callback expectations.

Attendant Console

  • Use Attendant Console for receptionist/operator style workflows.
  • Confirm the user is licensed and enabled in Control Hub.
  • Validate monitored users, calling behavior, transfer workflows, and browser compatibility.
  • Document which users or departments should appear in the console.

Call quality fundamentals

  • Collect caller, callee, timestamp, timezone, device, network, and symptom.
  • Separate signaling/routing problems from media quality problems.
  • Common quality symptoms include jitter, delay, packet loss, dropped calls, robotic audio, no audio, and one-way audio.
  • Compare wired vs Wi-Fi, Webex App vs desk phone, and office network vs mobile hotspot when isolating issues.

Contact Center

Contact Center setup workflow

  • Confirm subscription, region, tenant, and licensing.
  • Complete setup in Control Hub.
  • Assign Contact Center roles and licenses.
  • Create users, teams, sites, profiles, queues, entry points, and flows.
  • Validate Agent Desktop, supervisor access, reporting, recordings, and call routing.
  • Run end-to-end test calls for each entry point and business scenario.

Roles and privileges

  • Contact Center administration depends on Control Hub roles and Contact Center privileges.
  • Validate whether the user needs full administration, provisioning administration, supervisor capabilities, reporting, or read-only access.
  • For troubleshooting access issues, confirm license, role, team assignment, and profile assignment.

Users, teams, and sites

  • Users need correct licensing and Contact Center assignment.
  • Teams define operational groupings for routing, management, and reporting.
  • Sites or equivalent organization structures should match operational support needs.
  • Document which supervisors manage each team and which queues route to them.

Profiles overview

  • Agent profiles control agent capabilities and experience.
  • Desktop profiles influence what the agent can see and do in Agent Desktop.
  • Multimedia profiles control channel capacity and interaction handling.
  • User profiles and skill/profile settings should be documented before troubleshooting routing problems.

Entry points

  • Entry points are the front door for customer interactions.
  • Document the phone number/DNIS, channel, business purpose, flow, queue path, and fallback behavior.
  • For support, confirm that the number actually reaches the expected entry point before troubleshooting the flow.

Queues

  • Queues hold contacts waiting for agents or routing decisions.
  • Document queue name, team/agent mapping, routing strategy, skills, overflow, callback, and reporting owner.
  • For abandoned-call issues, check business hours, queue treatment, staffing, routing logic, and reporting definitions.

Flows

  • Flows define customer experience and routing logic.
  • Document prompts, menus, variables, queues, transfers, error handling, timeouts, retries, and closed-hours logic.
  • Keep a test plan for each flow: open, closed, holiday, invalid input, no input, transfer fail, queue fail, callback, and emergency routing.

Business hours and holidays

  • Business-hour logic should be documented outside the flow as a support reference.
  • Confirm timezone, holidays, special closures, and emergency closure handling.
  • Test closed routing before go-live and before major holidays.

Agent Desktop

  • Validate supported browser, login URL, user license, team, profile, dial number, and audio permissions.
  • Common issues include login failure, stuck state, no calls, wrong wrap-up codes, browser pop-up blocking, and audio device problems.
  • Collect screenshots and timestamps for desktop issues.

Supervisor workflows

  • Supervisors need correct privileges and team visibility.
  • Validate monitoring, coaching, reporting, queue visibility, team assignments, and real-time dashboards.
  • When a supervisor cannot see agents or queues, compare their assigned privileges and team scope with a working supervisor.

Analyzer and reporting

  • Analyzer provides stock and custom reporting for Contact Center operations.
  • Define metric owners and agreed business definitions for handled, abandoned, presented, service level, ASA, AHT, occupancy, and wrap-up reporting.
  • For report disputes, confirm timezone, filter, queue, team, channel, interval, and whether the metric definition matches the customer expectation.

Recordings and transcripts

  • Document recording policy, retention expectation, access roles, and compliance requirements.
  • For missing recordings, collect contact ID/call example, agent, queue, timestamp, and whether recording was expected for that interaction.
  • Validate whether the issue is policy, permission, availability, retention, or search criteria.

Callbacks

  • Document when callback is offered, queue threshold, customer experience, retries, caller ID, and reporting expectations.
  • Test callback during normal hours, near closing time, and high-volume conditions.
  • For callback complaints, confirm whether the customer selected callback, whether the callback was created, and whether an agent accepted it.

Digital channels

  • Document each enabled digital channel, routing path, team ownership, hours, and templates.
  • Confirm whether issues are channel provider, Contact Center routing, agent desktop visibility, or customer expectation.
  • Validate channel-specific capacity and multimedia profile settings.

Operational governance

  • Maintain a configuration workbook for entry points, queues, flows, teams, profiles, numbers, schedules, and reporting owners.
  • Use change windows for flow or routing changes.
  • After changes, run a standard test call matrix and document results.
  • Keep a rollback plan for every major change.

Devices

Device categories

  • MPP desk phones: Cisco multiplatform phones used for Webex Calling users and workspaces.
  • ATA devices: Analog telephone adapters used for analog phones, fax machines, overhead paging, and other analog endpoints.
  • DECT and wireless phones: Used for mobile staff, warehouses, medical offices, retail, and field-style environments.
  • Board, Desk, and Room Series: Video collaboration devices registered to Webex and managed through Control Hub.
  • Room kits/codecs: Room Kit, Room Kit Mini, Room Kit Plus, Room Kit Pro, Room Kit EQ/EQX, Codec Pro, and related camera/microphone/controller deployments.
  • Workspaces: Shared room or common-area device assignments in Control Hub.

User device vs workspace device

  • User device: Assigned to a person. Best for personal desk phones, Webex App calling, and assigned endpoints.
  • Workspace device: Assigned to a place. Best for conference rooms, huddle rooms, front desks, lobbies, warehouses, breakrooms, and shared phones.
  • Support rule: Always confirm whether the device is assigned to a user or workspace before troubleshooting number, extension, voicemail, and emergency-location behavior.
  • Documentation: Track device owner, location, number, extension, MAC address, serial number, room name, and support contact.

Activation and onboarding methods

  • MAC address assignment: Common for Webex Calling desk phones and ATAs. Add the device in Control Hub and assign it to the correct user/workspace.
  • Activation code: Common for Board, Desk, Room Series, and some device onboarding workflows. Generate the code in Control Hub and enter it on the device.
  • EDOS/automatic onboarding: Some devices, including ATA 191/192, can automatically onboard when provisioned by MAC address and factory booted/reset.
  • Manual configuration: Use only when the normal onboarding flow is not available or the official guide requires it.

ATA devices: what they are used for

  • ATAs connect analog endpoints to Webex Calling, such as analog phones, fax machines, overhead paging, and other analog endpoints.
  • Common Webex Calling ATA models include Cisco ATA 191 and ATA 192, depending on support and availability.
  • Always confirm whether the analog endpoint needs voice, fax, paging, DTMF, caller ID, or emergency calling behavior.
  • Document the physical analog port, assigned number/extension, location, emergency address, and connected analog equipment.

ATA troubleshooting checklist

  • Confirm the ATA model, MAC address, Control Hub assignment, and location.
  • Confirm the ATA has power, Ethernet, IP address, DNS, gateway, and internet access.
  • Confirm the analog endpoint is plugged into the correct ATA port.
  • For no dial tone, test with a known-good analog phone directly connected to the ATA.
  • For fax issues, confirm the fax machine settings, speed, error correction mode, and line quality expectations.
  • For paging issues, confirm whether the paging amplifier expects FXO/FXS behavior and whether a separate paging adapter is involved.
  • Reboot the ATA after Control Hub changes. Factory reset only after confirming assignment and network basics.

Room Kit Pro / Codec Pro overview

  • Room Kit Pro is built around Codec Pro and is intended for larger or more complex rooms where external cameras, microphones, speakers, displays, and AV integration may be required.
  • Use it for boardrooms, training rooms, divisible rooms, executive rooms, and spaces requiring advanced camera and microphone layouts.
  • Typical components include Codec Pro, cameras, microphones, speakers, displays, and a touch controller such as Room Navigator.
  • Document display count, camera model, microphone type, loudspeaker path, control system, network port, VLAN, IP address, and room calendar integration.

Room Kit EQ / Codec EQ overview

  • Room Kit EQ and Codec EQ are designed for modern room collaboration scenarios with intelligent video experiences and flexible room deployments.
  • They may be used in larger rooms where advanced camera framing, multiple screens, quality microphones, and modern RoomOS experiences are needed.
  • For advanced cinematic camera experiences such as Cross-view, verify the exact supported hardware combination, cameras, microphones, and RoomOS requirements.
  • Document camera placement, microphone placement, screen layout, controller pairing, and supported meeting platforms.

Room device selection guide

  • Desk/Desk Mini/Desk Pro: Personal desk or executive office video endpoint.
  • Board Series: Collaboration spaces needing whiteboarding, touch interaction, and video meetings.
  • Room Bar / Room Bar Pro: Small to medium rooms where an integrated bar-style device fits the space.
  • Room Kit / Room Kit Mini: Huddle or small rooms using external display setups.
  • Room Kit Plus: Medium to larger rooms needing stronger camera and audio options.
  • Room Kit Pro / Codec Pro: Large or complex rooms with AV integration, multiple screens, cameras, and microphones.
  • Room Kit EQ / Codec EQ: Modern larger rooms needing advanced RoomOS experiences and supported intelligent camera layouts.

RoomOS software and controller notes

  • RoomOS behavior and features change over time, so always check release notes before assuming feature availability.
  • Codec Pro-based systems such as Room Kit Pro, Room 70 G2, and Room Panorama have RoomOS 26 support requirements tied to Room Navigator usage; Touch 10 can affect upgrade behavior.
  • Before upgrading production rooms, document current software version, controller type, connected peripherals, and rollback/support plan.
  • After upgrades, test join, share, camera, microphone, speaker, mute, volume, calendar, and touch controller behavior.

Room Navigator and touch controllers

  • Room Navigator can be used as an in-room touch controller or room scheduling device depending on deployment.
  • Confirm whether the controller is directly paired, network paired, or configured for scheduling.
  • For pairing issues, check network reachability, device registration, software compatibility, and whether the controller is assigned to the correct room system.
  • Document controller location, pairing type, IP/MAC information, and whether it is required for RoomOS upgrade eligibility.

Content sharing options

  • Board, Desk, and Room Series devices can support wired sharing through HDMI or USB-C depending on device and room design.
  • Wireless sharing depends on device capability, Webex App behavior, network configuration, and enabled admin settings.
  • For content sharing issues, test cable, adapter, laptop output resolution, input selection, Webex App proximity/discovery, and network path.
  • Document which input should be used for guest laptop sharing and whether users should share locally, in-call, or wirelessly.

Room setup best practices

  • Start with room size, table shape, seating layout, screen placement, lighting, acoustics, and camera sight lines.
  • Place microphones based on seating positions, not just table convenience.
  • Avoid bright backlighting behind participants and avoid placing cameras too high or too far from the active speaker area.
  • For large rooms, validate audio pickup, speaker coverage, camera framing, and display readability from every seat.
  • Document final cable paths, IP/network details, display inputs, microphone locations, and supported room modes.

Remote access and remote support

  • Remote access can help support Board, Desk, and Room Series devices without physically being in the room.
  • Confirm the device is Webex cloud registered and reachable before starting a remote access session.
  • Use remote access for support tasks such as checking configuration, validating status, and assisting users, while respecting customer privacy and change-control rules.
  • For customer support, announce when remote access is being used and avoid making undocumented changes.

Network requirements checklist

  • Confirm DNS, NTP, HTTPS, WebSocket, media, and required Webex service access.
  • For Webex Calling phones, validate the Webex Calling port reference and network requirements.
  • For RoomOS devices behind a proxy, confirm supported proxy configuration and whether HTTPS/WebSocket traffic is allowed.
  • For intermittent issues, compare the device on the production VLAN, a test VLAN, and a known-good internet connection if possible.
  • Document VLAN, switch port, DHCP scope, DNS servers, proxy, firewall policy, and QoS behavior.

General device troubleshooting checklist

  • Confirm exact device model, serial number, MAC address, firmware/RoomOS version, and Control Hub assignment.
  • Confirm whether the device is assigned to a user or workspace.
  • Confirm location, number/extension, emergency address, and license where applicable.
  • Check power, Ethernet, IP address, gateway, DNS, NTP, proxy, and firewall access.
  • Check whether the device is online, offline, registered, partially configured, or waiting for activation.
  • Reboot after config changes and document the result.
  • Factory reset only when you have confirmed the device can be reactivated and you have the correct assignment information.

Device inventory template

  • Customer / organization.
  • Location and room name.
  • Device type and model.
  • Serial number and MAC address.
  • Assigned user or workspace.
  • Phone number and extension.
  • Emergency location.
  • Network VLAN / switch port.
  • RoomOS or firmware version.
  • Controller type: Room Navigator, Touch 10, phone sidecar, or none.
  • Peripherals: cameras, microphones, speakers, displays, paging adapter, fax machine, headset, or expansion module.
  • Support notes and last reviewed date.

Meeting issue intake

  • Collect meeting link or meeting number.
  • Identify host, affected participant, date, time, timezone, and error message.
  • Confirm Webex App version or browser.
  • Capture screenshot and whether the issue is join, audio, video, sharing, recording, or calendar.

Join issue checklist

  • Try the Webex App and browser join.
  • Confirm the meeting link is current.
  • Confirm the user is not joining too early or using an expired invite.
  • Check network, proxy, VPN, and endpoint security restrictions.
  • Try another device or network for isolation.

Audio and video checklist

  • Confirm microphone, speaker, camera, and browser permissions.
  • Check default audio device and headset selection.
  • Test computer audio and alternate audio options.
  • Close applications that may be holding the camera or microphone.
  • Confirm device drivers and OS permissions.

Calendar and join button

  • Confirm calendar integration is enabled.
  • Confirm the invite contains a valid Webex meeting link.
  • Confirm room or device calendar accepted the invite.
  • Check timezone, organizer, and meeting start time.
  • For room devices, validate calendar service and room mailbox behavior.

Meraki overview

  • Cisco Meraki is a cloud-managed networking platform used to deploy, monitor, and manage security appliances, switches, access points, cellular gateways, cameras, sensors, and endpoint management.
  • Most daily administration happens in the Meraki Dashboard.
  • The primary management hierarchy is organization, network, device inventory, devices, clients, policies, alerts, firmware, and administrators.
  • For support, always identify the organization, network, device serial number, client MAC/IP, timestamp, and recent configuration changes.

Dashboard structure

  • Organization: Top-level customer or administrative boundary.
  • Network: Logical container for devices and configuration. Networks are usually built per site, campus, branch, or functional area.
  • Inventory: Claimed hardware and licenses available to the organization.
  • Tags: Useful for filtering devices, clients, alerts, and API automation.
  • Administrators: Dashboard users with organization-wide or network-specific permissions.

Organization and network best practices

  • Use consistent network names, such as City - Site Name or Customer - Location - Function.
  • Document site address, WAN circuits, VLANs, public IPs, device serials, license status, firmware track, and support owner.
  • Use configuration templates for large standardized deployments, but document which settings are bound to the template and which are locally overridden.
  • Avoid making undocumented changes directly in production networks during active incidents unless the change is required for service restoration.

Licensing basics

  • Meraki environments require active licensing for managed devices and features.
  • Know whether the organization uses co-termination licensing, per-device licensing, or subscription-style behavior where applicable.
  • MX licensing commonly affects security and SD-WAN feature availability, so validate the license level before troubleshooting missing features.
  • Track license expiration, renewal owner, claimed devices, unclaimed devices, and retired hardware.

Firmware management

  • Firmware upgrades should be scheduled, communicated, and validated like production changes.
  • Review firmware release notes before upgrading MX, MS, MR, MG, MV, or MT devices.
  • For large organizations, test upgrades on a pilot site before broad deployment.
  • Document maintenance window, expected impact, rollback options, validation tests, and affected networks.
  • After upgrade, check device connectivity, client impact, VPN status, switching, wireless health, and alert history.

MX Security & SD-WAN

  • MX appliances provide routing, firewall, VPN, SD-WAN, traffic shaping, content filtering, and security features depending on licensing and model.
  • Document WAN1/WAN2 circuits, public IPs, VLANs, DHCP scopes, static routes, firewall rules, NAT rules, VPN peers, and traffic shaping policies.
  • For support, identify whether the issue is WAN connectivity, LAN routing, DHCP, DNS, firewall, NAT, VPN, SD-WAN policy, or content filtering.
  • Use event logs, appliance status, uplink statistics, clients, and packet capture when isolating issues.

MX WAN troubleshooting

  • Confirm whether WAN1, WAN2, cellular, or both wired uplinks are affected.
  • Check physical link, modem/ONT status, public IP assignment, DNS, gateway reachability, and Dashboard uplink status.
  • Compare Meraki uplink loss/latency with ISP test results.
  • For failover issues, confirm uplink preferences, SD-WAN policy, VPN path behavior, and cellular failover configuration.
  • Collect timestamps, circuit IDs, public IPs, packet loss, latency, traceroutes, and screenshots for carrier escalation.

VPN and AutoVPN

  • AutoVPN simplifies site-to-site VPN between Meraki MX networks in the same organization.
  • Document hub/spoke design, subnets advertised, VPN participation, route priorities, and firewall rules.
  • For non-Meraki VPN peers, document peer public IP, IKE/IPsec settings, pre-shared key handling, encryption/integrity, lifetime, and local/remote subnets.
  • For VPN issues, confirm public IP changes, NAT traversal, overlapping subnets, route availability, and firewall policy.

Firewall and NAT

  • Document Layer 3 firewall rules, Layer 7 rules, content filtering, IDS/IPS, port forwards, one-to-one NAT, one-to-many NAT, and site-to-site VPN firewall rules.
  • Rule order matters, so document why each rule exists and who approved it.
  • For blocked traffic, collect source IP, destination IP, port, protocol, client, VLAN, rule match, and timestamp.
  • Use packet capture and event logs to validate whether traffic reaches the MX and whether return traffic is present.

MS Switching

  • MS switches provide cloud-managed access, aggregation, and switching features depending on model.
  • Document switch model, serial, stack membership, uplinks, VLANs, trunk ports, access ports, PoE budget, link aggregation, STP root, and port schedules.
  • For endpoint issues, confirm port status, VLAN, cable test, PoE draw, port profile, authentication policy, and switch event log.
  • Use port descriptions consistently so support can identify connected devices quickly.

Switch port troubleshooting

  • Confirm the device is connected to the expected switch and port.
  • Check port status, negotiated speed/duplex, VLAN, trunk/access mode, allowed VLANs, STP state, and PoE status.
  • Run a cable test where supported and compare with physical cabling observations.
  • Move the device to a known-good port to determine whether the issue follows the endpoint, cable, port, or VLAN.
  • Document switch name, port number, endpoint MAC, IP, VLAN, and symptoms.

MR Wireless

  • MR access points provide cloud-managed wireless networking, SSIDs, RF profiles, client visibility, splash pages, and security controls.
  • Document SSIDs, VLAN tagging, authentication method, PSKs, RADIUS servers, RF profile, minimum bitrate, band steering, and traffic shaping.
  • For wireless issues, identify whether the problem is association, authentication, DHCP, DNS, roaming, RF coverage, interference, or upstream network access.
  • Use client timeline, event log, RF health, channel utilization, and packet capture when troubleshooting.

Wireless troubleshooting checklist

  • Collect client MAC, username, SSID, AP name, location, time, and symptom.
  • Confirm whether the client can see the SSID, associate, authenticate, receive DHCP, resolve DNS, and reach internal/external resources.
  • Check AP status, channel utilization, interference, signal strength, and roaming history.
  • Compare a known-good client on the same SSID and location.
  • Validate VLAN tagging, DHCP scope, firewall rules, group policies, and RADIUS logs.

MG Cellular Gateways

  • MG gateways provide cellular connectivity for primary or backup WAN use cases.
  • Document carrier, SIM/eSIM, APN, signal strength, antenna placement, data plan, connected MX or downstream device, and failover design.
  • For cellular issues, check signal, carrier outage, SIM activation, data usage, APN settings, cabling, and Dashboard status.
  • For backup WAN, test failover before relying on it during an outage.

MV Smart Cameras

  • MV cameras provide cloud-managed video, local storage, motion search, and camera analytics features depending on model and licensing.
  • Document camera placement, field of view, retention setting, quality setting, privacy requirements, network switch port, and access permissions.
  • For missing video, check camera online status, retention window, time range, motion search filters, storage health, and user permissions.
  • Camera deployments should consider privacy, physical security, legal/compliance expectations, and role-based access.

MT Sensors

  • MT sensors monitor environmental or physical conditions such as temperature, humidity, water leaks, door open/close state, and other sensor-specific conditions.
  • Document sensor type, gateway/AP relationship, placement, alert thresholds, notification recipients, and battery status.
  • For sensor issues, check last check-in, battery, gateway connectivity, placement, alert rules, and whether thresholds are configured correctly.
  • Test alert delivery after installation and after threshold changes.

Systems Manager

  • Systems Manager is Meraki endpoint management for mobile devices and computers.
  • Use it for device enrollment, profiles, apps, restrictions, certificates, compliance, owners, and inventory management.
  • For enrollment issues, confirm platform, ownership model, enrollment URL/profile, user, network access, and required certificates or management permissions.
  • Document device owner, serial, OS version, management state, compliance state, installed profiles, and last check-in.

Monitoring, alerts, and logging

  • Use Dashboard alerts for device offline, WAN changes, VPN issues, rogue APs, camera alerts, sensor thresholds, and other operational events.
  • Document alert recipients and escalation paths.
  • For integrations, consider webhooks, syslog, SNMP, API polling, or third-party monitoring depending on the use case.
  • Review alert noise regularly so important alerts are not ignored.

Meraki Dashboard API

  • The Meraki Dashboard API is a RESTful API for programmatically managing and monitoring Meraki networks at scale.
  • Common use cases include inventory reporting, bulk changes, network provisioning, device monitoring, license reporting, configuration audits, and role-based access control workflows.
  • Use API keys or supported bearer authentication methods according to current Meraki developer documentation.
  • For production scripts, add pagination, rate-limit handling, retries, logging, and change-control safeguards.

Meraki troubleshooting tools

  • Event log: Useful for configuration events, client events, VPN events, wireless events, and security events.
  • Clients page: Useful for tracking MAC/IP, usage, policy, VLAN, connected AP/switch, and client history.
  • Packet capture: Useful for validating whether traffic is present and where it stops.
  • Topology: Useful for identifying uplinks, downstream devices, and physical/logical relationships.
  • Cable test: Useful for switch-port physical layer checks where supported.
  • Live tools: Useful for ping, throughput, blink LEDs, and other device-specific checks.

  • Client MAC/IP/hostname if a client is affected.
  • Exact timestamps with timezone.
  • Expected behavior vs actual behavior.
  • Recent configuration changes or firmware upgrades.
  • Screenshots, packet captures, event log entries, carrier ticket numbers, ISP test results, or RADIUS/server logs where relevant.
  • Business impact and urgency.

APIs

Authentication options

  • Personal access tokens are useful for testing and exploration.
  • OAuth integrations are used when an app acts on behalf of a Webex user.
  • Bots are useful for messaging-space automation.
  • Service apps can be used for organization-level Contact Center integrations where supported.
  • Never hardcode production secrets in browser-side code.

Authorization header

  • Most Webex API calls require a bearer token.
  • Use the exact format: Authorization: Bearer ACCESS_TOKEN.
  • For troubleshooting, log response code, request ID if available, endpoint, and scope context without exposing tokens.

Scopes and permissions

  • Scopes define what the token can request.
  • Admin APIs may also require the authenticating user to have the correct admin role.
  • A token can be valid but still fail if the user lacks the role, license, or org access.
  • For integrations, document required scopes and why each one is needed.

OAuth checklist

  • Redirect URI must exactly match the developer portal configuration.
  • Authorization code can only be used once.
  • Client secret must be current.
  • Refresh token handling must be secure.
  • State parameter should be used to protect the OAuth flow.
  • Token errors should be logged safely.

Webhooks

  • Use webhooks to receive event notifications instead of polling.
  • Validate webhook target URL, secret, event type, and app authorization.
  • Build retry-safe handlers that can handle duplicate events.
  • Store only necessary payload fields and avoid logging sensitive data.

Common API errors

  • 401 means token missing, expired, malformed, or invalid.
  • 403 means token is accepted but lacks scope, role, license, or permission.
  • 404 often means the resource ID, org ID, or endpoint is wrong or inaccessible.
  • 409 usually indicates conflict or duplicate resource behavior.
  • 429 means rate limit; use backoff and retry logic.

Contact Center integrations

  • Document tenant, org, scopes, service app/integration model, endpoint purpose, data flow, and failure handling.
  • For reporting or CDR pulls, agree on timezone, fields, retention, and polling frequency.
  • For CRM screen pops or logging, define exactly what data is written and when.

Calling integrations

  • Calling APIs and webhooks can support call-control and workflow automation use cases.
  • Confirm whether the target use case requires user authorization, admin authorization, or another app model.
  • For production, add audit logging, token rotation, error handling, and rate-limit handling.

Universal triage checklist

  • Who is affected?
  • What exactly is failing?
  • When did it start?
  • Was anything changed?
  • Is it one user, many users, one site, one queue, one device type, or all users?
  • Can the issue be reproduced on demand?
  • What is the business impact?

Calling: one-way/no audio

  • Confirm inbound, outbound, or both.
  • Collect caller, called party, time, timezone, device, and network.
  • Test wired vs Wi-Fi and desk phone vs Webex App.
  • Check headset and audio permissions.
  • Review network/firewall/QoS path and Control Hub call details if available.

Calling: wrong routing

  • Identify the dialed number.
  • Map each hop: number, auto attendant, schedule, queue, hunt group, user, voicemail, external transfer, or Contact Center.
  • Check holiday, night service, operating mode, forced forwarding, and user-level forwarding.
  • Place a controlled test call and document the actual path.

Contact Center: agent not receiving calls

  • Confirm agent login state and availability.
  • Confirm team, profile, multimedia profile, capacity, skills, and queue assignment.
  • Confirm entry point routes to the expected flow and queue.
  • Check whether other agents in the same team receive calls.
  • Collect a call example and flow path.

Contact Center: flow issue

  • Confirm the DNIS and entry point.
  • Confirm the published flow version.
  • Test each branch: open, closed, holiday, timeout, invalid input, queue fail, transfer fail, callback, and emergency routing.
  • Review variables, prompts, queue references, and fallback paths.

Agent Desktop: login issue

  • Confirm license, role, team, and desktop profile.
  • Confirm browser compatibility and clear stale sessions if needed.
  • Check pop-up, microphone, and notification permissions.
  • Capture exact error, screenshot, timestamp, and user ID.

Devices: phone not registering

  • Confirm MAC, model, assignment, location, and activation state.
  • Confirm IP, gateway, DNS, and internet access.
  • Try another port/cable/network.
  • Reboot after assignment changes.
  • Avoid factory reset until assignment and network basics are validated.

Meetings: cannot join

  • Confirm meeting link/number, date, time, timezone, and host.
  • Try browser and Webex App.
  • Check audio/video/browser permissions.
  • Test another network/device.
  • Capture screenshot and error text.

API: 401/403

  • Confirm Authorization header.
  • Confirm token validity and expiration.
  • Confirm scopes.
  • Confirm user role/license/org access.
  • Confirm resource ID belongs to the accessible org.
  • Check whether the app model is correct for the endpoint.

Escalation packet

  • Customer/org name and org ID if available.
  • Affected users, numbers, queues, devices, meetings, or integrations.
  • Exact examples with timestamps and timezone.
  • Expected vs actual behavior.
  • Recent changes.
  • Business impact and urgency.
  • Screenshots, logs, HAR files, device logs, call examples, contact IDs, or API responses where applicable.

Clerk Chat overview

  • Clerk Chat enables business messaging such as SMS, MMS, RCS, and WhatsApp for collaboration platforms including Webex, Microsoft Teams, and Zoom.
  • For Webex customers, the value is enabling messaging workflows on business phone numbers so teams can communicate with customers from tools they already use.
  • Common use cases include appointment confirmations, customer service conversations, sales follow-up, review requests, campaigns, reminders, and AI-assisted responses.
  • Support should always confirm the customer platform, enabled numbers, messaging channel, users, groups, compliance status, and exact symptom before escalating.

Customer setup process

  • Collect customer company name, primary admin, billing/contact email, business address, website, and support contact.
  • Confirm target platform: Webex, Microsoft Teams, Zoom, or another supported deployment path.
  • Collect the phone numbers to enable for messaging and identify whether they are local numbers, toll-free numbers, shared lines, queues, auto attendants, or direct user numbers.
  • Confirm whether the customer needs one-to-one texting, shared inbox/team texting, campaigns, AI automation, CRM integration, or all of the above.
  • Confirm compliance needs before sending production messages, especially 10DLC/toll-free verification and opt-in/opt-out language.
  • Schedule a validation session to test inbound messages, outbound messages, attachments, notifications, user access, and escalation workflow.

Pre-onboarding checklist

  • Customer legal business name.
  • Business address and website.
  • Primary admin name, email, and phone number.
  • List of numbers to enable for messaging.
  • Proof of number ownership or current carrier/account information if requested by the provider.
  • Desired users, departments, shared inboxes, and access groups.
  • Messaging use cases: support, sales, reminders, reviews, collections, appointment confirmations, or campaigns.
  • Expected monthly message volume and whether messages are conversational or campaign-style.
  • Required integrations such as Salesforce, HubSpot, Outlook, Dynamics, PipeDrive, or other CRM/helpdesk systems.

Webex setup workflow

  • Confirm the Webex organization and target users.
  • Confirm which Webex Calling numbers should be enabled for messaging.
  • Install or authorize the Clerk Chat application according to the Clerk Chat/Webex App Hub instructions.
  • Map enabled numbers to the correct users, teams, shared inboxes, or departments.
  • Test inbound SMS, outbound SMS, MMS/media, notifications, and user permissions.
  • Document which Webex numbers are messaging-enabled and who owns each inbox operationally.

Number enablement checklist

  • Confirm the number is active and owned by the customer.
  • Confirm whether voice service should remain with the current provider while messaging is enabled separately.
  • Confirm whether the number is local, toll-free, or mobile because messaging registration requirements may differ.
  • Confirm the number is not already text-enabled with another provider or messaging platform.
  • For shared business numbers, define who receives messages and how ownership is handled.
  • Test inbound and outbound messaging after enablement before announcing the number to customers.

10DLC, toll-free verification, and compliance

  • Business texting may require brand/campaign registration, toll-free verification, opt-in handling, opt-out handling, and approved message use cases.
  • Collect accurate legal business information, tax/business identifiers when required, website, sample messages, privacy policy/terms links, and opt-in method.
  • Message content should match the approved use case. Avoid sending unrelated campaign traffic from a conversational support number.
  • Document STOP/START/HELP behavior and customer consent expectations.
  • If messages are blocked after approval, collect message ID if available, sending number, receiving number, carrier, timestamp, content sample, and registration status.

Common features

  • SMS/MMS: One-to-one and team messaging with support for media where available.
  • RCS: Rich messaging features where supported by carrier, device, and product configuration.
  • WhatsApp: Customer messaging channel where enabled and configured.
  • Shared inboxes: Team-based message ownership and collaboration.
  • Campaigns: Broadcast or bulk-style messaging, subject to compliance and product limits.
  • AI workflows: Automated or assisted responses, summaries, routing, and customer-service productivity use cases.
  • CRM integrations: Sync or log conversations with systems such as Salesforce, HubSpot, Outlook, Dynamics, and PipeDrive where supported.

Operational configuration

  • Define business hours, after-hours responses, team ownership, and escalation behavior.
  • Create approved templates for common messages such as appointment confirmation, missed call follow-up, review request, and support acknowledgement.
  • Define who can send campaigns, export data, manage contacts, and administer numbers.
  • Document whether messages should be assigned manually, automatically routed, or handled by a shared queue/team.
  • Review opt-in/opt-out handling before go-live.

Go-live testing checklist

  • Send an outbound SMS to a mobile test number.
  • Reply inbound from the mobile number and confirm the right user/team sees it.
  • Test MMS/media if required.
  • Test notifications in the chosen platform.
  • Test shared inbox assignment and handoff.
  • Test STOP, START, and HELP behavior if applicable.
  • Test a real customer workflow such as appointment reminder, review request, support question, or sales follow-up.
  • Document results and customer approval before full launch.

Troubleshooting: messages not sending

  • Confirm the sending number is enabled and active in Clerk Chat.
  • Confirm the user has permission to send from that number or inbox.
  • Confirm the destination number is valid and can receive SMS/MMS/RCS/WhatsApp for the selected channel.
  • Check whether the message content violates campaign, carrier, compliance, attachment, or length rules.
  • Check 10DLC/toll-free verification status and whether the use case is approved.
  • Collect sender number, recipient number, timestamp, message content sample, channel, error, and screenshot.

Troubleshooting: inbound messages not received

  • Confirm the number is text-enabled and mapped to the correct inbox/team/user.
  • Confirm the sender is texting the correct number.
  • Check whether the sending carrier reports the message as delivered.
  • Check if the message is going to a different inbox, archived view, filtered conversation, or blocked contact.
  • Confirm platform notifications and browser/mobile app permissions.
  • Escalate with sender number, recipient number, timestamp, carrier if known, and screenshots.

Troubleshooting: Webex integration or login issue

  • Confirm the user can sign in to Webex and Clerk Chat.
  • Confirm the Clerk Chat app is authorized/installed for the intended users.
  • Confirm browser, cache, cookies, pop-ups, and third-party cookie restrictions if using a browser experience.
  • Compare affected user permissions with a working user.
  • Capture exact error message, user email, timestamp, browser, platform, and screenshots.

Troubleshooting: carrier filtering or blocked messages

  • Confirm registration/verification status for the brand, campaign, or toll-free number.
  • Confirm message content matches the approved use case.
  • Check for URL shorteners, high-risk phrases, missing opt-out language, or unexpected message volume spikes.
  • Collect examples across multiple recipient carriers if possible.
  • Escalate with sender, recipient, carrier, timestamp, message sample, error, and registration status.

  • Messaging channel: SMS, MMS, RCS, or WhatsApp.
  • Exact timestamp and timezone.
  • Message content sample, attachment type, or campaign name.
  • Registration/verification status if relevant.
  • Error message, screenshots, and business impact.

FirstDigital overview

  • FirstDigital provides enterprise voice services including SIP trunking, Microsoft Teams Direct Routing as a Service, Zoom Phone BYOC, and Cloud Connected PSTN options for Webex Calling and Webex Contact Center.
  • For support, identify whether the issue is PSTN/carrier, SIP signaling, number routing, porting, Webex configuration, Contact Center routing, customer network, or endpoint behavior.
  • Always collect the customer, service type, numbers, call examples, timestamps, direction, and recent changes before escalation.

What is needed for a number port

  • Completed Letter of Authorization (LOA) signed by the authorized signer.
  • Customer legal business name exactly as it appears with the losing carrier.
  • Service address associated with the numbers. Avoid PO Box addresses unless the carrier specifically accepts them.
  • Current carrier name.
  • Account number.
  • Billing telephone number (BTN) if applicable.
  • Port-out PIN or passcode when required, especially for wireless/mobile numbers and some VoIP providers.
  • Recent invoice or bill copy showing account name, service address, account number, and numbers to port.
  • Complete list of numbers to port.
  • List of numbers that should remain with the losing carrier if this is a partial port.
  • Desired requested port date and time window.
  • Contact name, email, and phone number for port coordination.

Porting checklist

  • Confirm all numbers are active with the losing carrier. Do not cancel service before the port completes.
  • Confirm number ownership and account details using the most recent bill or CSR.
  • Separate local, toll-free, wireless, and complex/project ports because requirements and timelines may differ.
  • Confirm whether this is a full port or partial port.
  • For partial ports, confirm what services and numbers will remain behind with the losing carrier.
  • Check for DSL, alarm, elevator, fax, paging, credit-card terminals, or other services tied to the numbers.
  • Confirm emergency service address and callback expectations after the port.
  • Build the numbers and routing in Webex/FirstDigital before the port date.
  • Test inbound, outbound, caller ID, emergency routing plan, Contact Center routing, and failover behavior after cutover.

CSR and rejection prevention

  • A Customer Service Record (CSR) is often the cleanest way to confirm exactly how the losing carrier has the account listed.
  • Common rejection causes include wrong account number, wrong service address, wrong authorized signer, missing PIN, locked/frozen numbers, inactive service, pending orders, and partial-port conflicts.
  • If available, request the CSR before submitting the port request for large or sensitive ports.
  • Make sure the LOA matches the losing carrier account details, not just the customer’s preferred business name.

Toll-free porting notes

  • Toll-free numbers may require toll-free-specific authorization and may involve RespOrg coordination.
  • Collect the toll-free number list, current provider, account information, LOA, bill copy, and desired routing destination.
  • Confirm whether toll-free voice and toll-free messaging are both being moved or only voice.
  • After cutover, test from multiple carriers if possible because toll-free routing may not update everywhere at the exact same time.

Wireless/mobile porting notes

  • Wireless numbers often require an account number, port-out PIN, transfer PIN, or account passcode.
  • Confirm the number is unlocked or eligible to transfer before submitting the port.
  • Wireless ports may be more time-sensitive once approved, so coordinate the cutover window closely with the customer.
  • Do not assume the billing address is the same as the service address on the carrier account.

Port project plan

  • Create a number inventory with current carrier, target carrier, destination, routing, user/queue/auto attendant/Contact Center entry point, and emergency location.
  • Prebuild numbers and routing where possible.
  • Schedule a cutover bridge for larger ports.
  • Assign roles: customer approver, FirstDigital/carrier contact, Webex admin, Contact Center admin, test callers, and rollback owner.
  • Prepare a test plan for inbound, outbound, toll-free, fax/ATA, Contact Center, after-hours, emergency plan, caller ID, and failover.
  • Document completion time, issues, and post-port validation results.

Troubleshooting: inbound calls failing

  • Confirm the caller number, called number, timestamp, timezone, and error heard by the caller.
  • Confirm whether the number has ported, is pending, or is still with the losing carrier.
  • Confirm number assignment and routing in Webex Calling or Contact Center.
  • Check whether the issue affects one DID, a block of numbers, toll-free, one location, or all numbers.
  • Test from multiple external carriers, such as mobile and landline, if possible.
  • Escalate with call examples, SIP traces if available, routing destination, and business impact.

Troubleshooting: outbound calls failing

  • Collect calling number, called number, timestamp, timezone, user/device, location, and error message or intercept recording.
  • Confirm whether the user can call internal extensions, local numbers, long distance, toll-free, and emergency/test numbers where appropriate.
  • Check caller ID configuration and whether the outbound caller ID is valid and assigned.
  • Confirm dialing format, permissions, international calling restrictions, and any toll restrictions.
  • Compare Webex Calling behavior with carrier/SIP trunk status.

Troubleshooting: call quality

  • Collect examples with caller, called party, date/time/timezone, direction, and symptom.
  • Separate carrier/PSTN quality from LAN/WAN/client quality by testing internal calls, PSTN calls, and different networks/devices.
  • Common symptoms include one-way audio, no audio, dropped calls, jitter, delay, robotic audio, and intermittent audio.
  • Collect network path details, device type, headset, Wi-Fi/wired status, ISP, and any firewall/QoS changes.
  • Escalate with call examples and packet/SIP captures where available.

Troubleshooting: fax, ATA, analog, or paging

  • Confirm whether the service is fax, analog phone, paging, alarm, elevator, or another analog use case.
  • Confirm ATA model, assigned number, physical port, and connected equipment.
  • Test with a known-good analog phone directly connected to the ATA.
  • For fax, check fax speed, ECM, line quality, and whether the destination fax works with other senders.
  • For paging, confirm expected signaling, adapter type, amplifier behavior, and whether DTMF is required.

Troubleshooting: port delayed or rejected

  • Check the rejection reason from the losing carrier.
  • Compare LOA, CSR, account number, service address, BTN, signer, and PIN.
  • Confirm there are no pending orders, account locks, freezes, inactive numbers, or partial-port restrictions.
  • Correct the rejected field only after confirming the source of truth with the customer/current carrier.
  • Document the revised submission date, expected FOC date, and customer impact.

Emergency calling / E911 checklist

  • Confirm the emergency service address for every location and number/user as required.
  • Confirm callback number behavior.
  • Confirm customer understands emergency calling limitations for remote users, soft clients, and moved devices.
  • For Webex Calling, validate emergency settings in Control Hub and the provider workflow.
  • Document any emergency calling test process according to approved procedures.
